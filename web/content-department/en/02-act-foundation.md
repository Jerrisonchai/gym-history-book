# Act II: National Foundation (1990s–2000s)

## 2.1 The Softgym Revolution

As the gymnastics troupe evolved toward the 1990s–2000s, the need for diversified performance became clear. Inspired by competitive gymnastics — particularly rhythmic gymnastics — the concept of **Softgym** emerged.

The idea began in 1989 when one committee member took the initiative to learn independently. By 1992, Softgym was formally established, with training beginning in small groups. To raise technical standards and proper teaching methods, select members regularly trained with professional gymnastics coaches externally, then returned to practice, share, and learn together with the team.

**The goal:** Transfer learned skills from seniors to juniors, generation after generation. External training took place at a national indoor gymnastics facility near Maktab Perguruan Teknik Cheras (now relocated to Bukit Jalil).

## 2.2 National Competitions Era (1994–2008)

With Softgym established, the team began collecting formation archives from across the country. To stimulate creativity, the **first national competition** was held in **1994** — designed to consolidate and unite gymnastic troupe members nationwide.

This period saw two pivotal turning points:

| Turning Point | Description |
|--------------|-------------|
| **First Turn** | Softgym concepts integrated into all performances, adding fluidity and elegance |
| **Second Turn** | Music integration — performances choreographed to song rhythm and tempo. Sabah led this breakthrough. |

From 1994 to 2008, annual gymnastic competitions were held. To spread Softgym across all states, seniors recorded every move and technique on **VHS tapes** and distributed them to each state chapter as reference material.

During this era, the troupe received performance invitations from all directions, including numerous government events. State-level performances gradually incorporated Softgym concepts into their routines.

## 2.3 What Made This Era Special

The greatest achievement of this era (alongside the 1980s) was the forging of the **Men's Division spirit** — grounded in the master-disciple bond (师弟不二), uniting every troupe member to express the organization's spirit through performance.

Leaders and coordinators visited members to encourage their growth. Every Sunday, they began their day with *Daimoku* before setting out on visits. Transportation was limited — most traveled by public buses and trains to reach members across the country.

This was the era of sacrifice, of personal visits, of VHS tapes passing hand to hand — building the national foundation one member at a time.
